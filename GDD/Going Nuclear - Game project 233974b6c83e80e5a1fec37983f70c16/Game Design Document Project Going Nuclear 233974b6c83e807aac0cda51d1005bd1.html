1. Vision & High Concept

Project NUCLEAR WAR is a turn-based, satirical grand strategy game for PC. It is a spiritual successor and homage to the classic 1989 card game "Nuclear War," redesigned for a modern audience and adapted to reflect today's complex geopolitical landscape.

The human player takes on the role of a world leader, confronting AI-controlled exaggerated caricatures of current global leaders. The core experience centers on resource management, building an arsenal through a card-based system, and employing diplomatic, military, economic, and information warfare tactics to become the last faction standing with a surviving population.

The game's tone is one of dark humor and satire. While the gameplay involves potentially world-ending scenarios, the presentation, character design, and event descriptions are designed to be humorous and critical. The game's central theme is the absurdity of global power struggles, grounded in real-world dynamics but amplified to caricature levels to create a sense of fun while retaining an underlying message about real-world consequences.

2. Core Gameplay Loop

The game progresses in turns, with each player completing their turn before the next player begins. A player's turn is structured into three phases:

  1. Start of Turn Phase (Upkeep):
    • The player's Action Points (AP) are reset to their faction's base value.
    • Resources are generated: Treasury is calculated based on current Population and Morale.
    • Any active Status Effects (e.g., "Fallout," "Sanctions") are processed.
    • A card is drawn from the player's deck.
  1. Action Phase:
    • The player spends their AP on a variety of actions until they have insufficient AP for any remaining moves or choose to end their turn.
    • The player can queue up one major offensive action per turn (a Conventional Attack or an InfoWar Attack) by selecting cards and a target.
  1. End of Turn Phase:
    • The player clicks the "End Turn" button.
    • If an offensive action was queued, it is resolved, and the results are displayed.
    • Control passes to the next player in the turn order. The human player's UI is disabled, and they become an observer.

2.1. Game Flow: The Two-Phase System

The game is divided into two major phases. The transition from Phase 1 to Phase 2 is a critical turning point in the game.

Phase 1: Deterrence & Escalation (The "Cold War")

This is the default starting phase of the game. The world is tense, but full-scale kinetic warfare is not yet an option. The focus is on building power, gathering intelligence, and manipulating the global stage.

  • Characteristics:
    • The global DEFCON level is relatively high (5, 4, or 3).
    • Direct conventional military attacks are either impossible or heavily penalized (e.g., cost double the AP, cause a massive relationship penalty with all other factions).
  • Primary Actions & Goals:
    • Economy & Technology: Use the Build action to increase your card arsenal and prepare for the future.
    • Espionage & Sabotage: Use UTILITY cards to spy on enemy hands, steal Treasury, or sabotage their Morale.
    • Information Warfare: Use INFO_WAR cards to directly attack an enemy's Population and Morale through non-military means.
    • Diplomacy & Alliances: Use diplomatic actions to form alliances, which provide defensive benefits and are crucial for the Diplomatic Victory condition. Manipulate relationships through targeted aid or targeted slander.
    • Saber-Rattling: Amass a visible arsenal of powerful DELIVERY and PAYLOAD cards to deter attacks from other players.
  • Phase Transition:
    • The game automatically transitions to Phase 2 the moment any player's action causes the global DEFCON level to drop to 2.

Phase 2: Kinetic & Annihilation (The "Hot War")

This phase begins when a line has been crossed and global tensions have reached a breaking point. The gloves come off, and the world descends into open conflict.

  • Characteristics:
    • The global DEFCON level is critically low (2 or 1).
    • The penalties for launching conventional military attacks are removed. It is now the primary method of conflict.
    • Negative global effects associated with low DEFCON are active (e.g., automatic morale loss each turn).
  • Primary Actions & Goals:
    • Annihilation: Use Conventional Attack actions (DELIVERY + PAYLOAD cards) to eliminate other factions' populations.
    • Continued Covert Ops: The tools from Phase 1 (INFO_WAR, Espionage, etc.) remain available and are crucial for softening up targets and defending against non-military threats.
    • Survival: The game becomes a desperate fight to be the last one standing or to achieve one of the victory conditions before you are wiped out.

3. Key Systems & Mechanics

3.1. Resource Management

  • Population: The core "health" of a faction. Victory is achieved by being the last player with Population > 0. It is a primary factor in Treasury generation.
  • Treasury (GDP): The main spendable currency. Used to pay for the "Build" action.
  • Morale (0-100%): A modifier that affects the efficiency of Treasury generation. High morale provides a bonus; low morale (caused by propaganda, attacks, or low DEFCON) incurs a penalty.
  • Action Points (AP): A per-turn resource used to perform actions. The base AP can vary by faction and be affected by game events, with a typical range of 2-5 AP per turn.

3.2. Primary Actions & Costs

  • Build (Cost: 1 AP + Treasury): The player spends resources to "build" technology/intelligence, drawing a set number of cards from their deck. This is the primary method of card acquisition.
  • Play Conventional Attack (Cost: 2 AP): The player selects one DELIVERY card, one PAYLOAD card, and one target enemy faction.
  • Play InfoWar Card (Cost: 1 AP): The player selects one INFO_WAR card and one target enemy faction.
  • Play Defense/Utility Card (Cost varies): Playing a defensive card or a card with a special utility effect (e.g., "Offer Alliance").

3.2.1. The "Directed Build" Action (Reworked)

The "Build" action is no longer a single button. It is now a menu of investment options. When the player initiates a "Build," they are presented with a choice of which sector of their military-industrial complex to fund.

  • Mechanic:
    1. The player clicks the [Button: Build].
    1. The [Deployment Screen] appears, but instead of cards, it shows a list of Investment Sectors.
    1. Each sector has a different Treasury and AP cost and yields cards of a specific type.
  • Example Investment Sectors:
    • "Nuclear Program" (High Cost): Yields PAYLOAD cards (Warheads). Allows for the creation of diversified warhead sizes.
    • "Aerospace & Ballistics" (High Cost): Yields DELIVERY cards (Missiles, Bombers).
    • "Cyber Warfare Division" (Medium Cost): Yields INFO_WAR cards.
    • "Intelligence Agency" (Medium Cost): Yields UTILITY cards (Espionage, Sabotage).
    • "Civil Defense Initiative" (Low Cost): Yields DEFENSE cards.
  • Strategic Impact: This gives the player direct control over their strategy. Do they rush for powerful warheads, or build up their intelligence network first? This is a core strategic choice. World Events can directly impact these sectors (e.g., "Sanctions on Nuclear Materials" increases the cost of the "Nuclear Program").

3.3. Faction Asymmetry

Each faction is unique, with characteristics grounded in a satirical view of their real-world counterpart. This is defined in their FactionData.tres file.

  • Unique Starting Conditions: Each faction has different base values for Population, Treasury, Morale, and AP.
  • Unique Abilities: Each faction has a unique passive or active ability that defines their playstyle (e.g., USA's economic power, China's demographic advantage).
  • Relationships: A numerical score (-10 to +10) defining their starting disposition towards other factions, directly influencing AI targeting priority.
    • 3.3.1. Player Role: The human player does not represent a specific faction. They are an abstract, "meta" entity controlling events. The player character has no population, treasury, or morale of their own. Their goal is to ensure the survival of at least one faction that is allied with them, or simply to be the "kingmaker" who decides the ultimate victor. (We can refine the victory condition later).
    • 3.3.2. Faction Roles: The 5 factions on the board are AI-controlled entities. They have their own stats, abilities, and relationships with each other. Their relationship with the human player starts at neutral and is modified by the player's direct actions against them or in their favor.

3.4. Global Systems

  • DEFCON Status (5 down to 1): A global "danger meter" that affects all players. It decreases with major attacks. Lower levels introduce negative global effects, such as increased costs, automatic morale loss, or the breaking of alliances at DEFCON 1.
  • World Events: At the start of each full round, a random event card is drawn. These events (e.g., "Solar Flare," "Global Pandemic," "Economic Summit") introduce unpredictable challenges or boons that can affect a single player, two players, or everyone on the board.

3.5. Card & Deck System

Cards are the primary tools through which players interact with the world and each other. Each player has their own deck and hand, drawing new cards primarily through the "Build" action.

3.5.1. Card Categories & Cross-Dependencies

  • DELIVERY: Defines how an attack is sent. Has a success_chance. Useless on its own.
  • PAYLOAD: Defines what is being sent. Has a damage value and/or special effects. Requires a DELIVERY card to be played.
  • INFO_WAR: A direct, non-kinetic attack on a faction's internal stability.
  • UTILITY: Covert, economic, and diplomatic actions.
  • DEFENSE: Used to counter or mitigate opponent actions. (Implementation detail: For now, these will be played on a player's own turn to grant a "shield" for one round, rather than as a reaction).

3.5.2. Weapon Diversification (Payloads)

To make the "Nuclear Program" investment meaningful, we will diversify warhead types as you suggested. Warheads will now have a Size (affecting damage) and a Scarcity (affecting build cost and probability).

  • Tactical Warhead (1-10 MT):
    • Damage: 1-10 Population.
    • Scarcity: Common. Cheap to build.
    • Use Case: For small, precise strikes or for finishing off a weakened opponent.
  • Strategic Warhead (20-50 MT):
    • Damage: 20-50 Population.
    • Scarcity: Uncommon. The standard workhorse warhead.
  • Multi-Megaton Warhead (100+ MT):
    • Damage: 100+ Population.
    • Scarcity: Rare. Very expensive and time-consuming to build (may cost more AP or require a special "Research" project first).
    • Use Case: A game-changing superweapon, designed to annihilate a major faction in a single blow. Its use would have severe diplomatic and DEFCON penalties.

3.6. Victory Conditions

There are two paths to victory in the game.

  • 1. Annihilation Victory (Default):
    • This is achieved when all other factions have been eliminated (their Population is 0).
    • If the Player's "Null Faction" is eliminated, the game ends in defeat for the player. The AI factions will continue to fight until one remains, and that faction is declared the winner.
  • 2. Diplomatic Victory (Difficult):
    • This victory can only be attempted during Phase 1 (Deterrence & Escalation). It becomes impossible once full-scale war breaks out.
    • Mechanic: The "Global Peace Summit" Action.
      • Cost: A very high amount of AP and Treasury (e.g., 5 AP, 500 Treasury). This represents the immense political and economic capital required to bring the world to the table.
      • Condition: The player must be in a formal alliance with at least two other major factions.
      • Resolution: When called, every surviving AI faction votes "Yes" or "No" on the peace treaty.

4. User Interface (UI) & User Experience (UX)

The game is presented through a custom UI designed to look and feel like a gritty, physical military console.

  • Three-Layer System:
    1. Background Layer (-1): Displays faction leader avatars and a dynamic world map, viewed as if on screens behind the console.
    1. Bezel Layer (0): A static overlay image of the console itself, which frames all other elements. This layer is ignored by the mouse.
    1. UI Layer (1): The top layer containing all interactive elements: player stats displays, action buttons, the game log, and pop-up menus like the CardChooser.
  • Interaction Flow: The player uses a clear, category-based system. They click an action button (e.g., "Delivery"), which opens a CardChooser pop-up displaying only the relevant cards from their hand. This minimizes clutter and streamlines decision-making.

4.1 - Core Interaction Flow

  • 4.1.1. Player Action: When the human player clicks a primary action button (e.g., "Delivery," "Payload," "InfoWar"), the game's state changes to "Targeting Mode."
  • 4.1.2. Deployment Screen: A dedicated UI panel, the Deployment Screen, animates into view from the bottom of the screen, appearing to slide up from under the main console bezel. This screen is the primary interface for selecting cards.
  • 4.1.3. Card Selection: The Deployment Screen displays all available cards of the chosen category (e.g., all DELIVERY cards in hand). It is composed of "Secondary Option" panels for lists of choices and a central "Card Visuals Overlay" to display a large, clear image and description of the currently hovered or selected card.
  • 4.1.4. Confirmation: Once a card is chosen from the Deployment Screen, the screen animates away, and the player's selection is registered. The main view returns to the world map/animations.

5. Technical Overview

  • Engine: Godot 4.x
  • Architecture: A highly modular, data-driven design.
    • Data Resources (.tres): All static data for factions (FactionData), cards (CardData), and players (PlayerState) are stored in custom resource files, allowing for easy editing and expansion without changing code.
    • Global Managers (Autoloads): Central singleton scripts (GameManager, CardDatabase, FactionDatabase, Logger) handle the game's state, rules, and data loading.
    • Reusable UI Scenes: UI components (PlayerPanel, CardChooser, PlaceholderButton) are built as self-contained, reusable scenes (.tscn).
